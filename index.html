Chapter 1 — Introduction to Wireless Communication Systems

Historical evolution · 1G→5G · Cellular principle · Frequency reuse · System architecture · Key requirements · Wireless challenges
🎯 Understand wireless system fundamentals · Trace cellular evolution from 1946 to 5G · Identify main wireless impairments and design constraints
Historical Evolution of Wireless Communications
📻 Early Beginnings (1946–1970s)
  • 1946: First mobile telephone system in St. Louis, USA — only 6 speech channels
  • Limited spectrum availability → birth of the cellular principle at AT&T Bell Labs
  • 1957: Soviet Union launched Sputnik → paving the way for satellite communications
  • 1979: Japan's NTT launched first commercial cellular system in Tokyo
📱 1G → 2G (1980s–1990s)
  • 1G (Analog): AMPS in USA, NMT in Europe — low penetration, bulky phones
  • Transition to digital → development of GSM (dominant 2G standard)
  • By 2000: mobile penetration exceeded 50% in Europe and Japan
  • USA & Japan had multiple competing 2G standards (standardization issues)
🚀 3G → 5G (2000s–today)
  • 3G: CDMA-based systems, data services, video calls
  • 4G/LTE (2009): dominant broadband standard, OFDM-based
  • 2006: WiMAX (IEEE 802.16m) introduced
  • 5G: real-time tactile internet, remote surgery, ultra-low latency (<1ms)
Wireless System Architecture
🏗️ Basic System Components
  • UE (User Equipment): connects to a Base Station (BS) via radio signal
  • BS (Base Station): connects to the Mobile Switching Center (MSC)
  • MSC: links to the internet and/or public telephone system (PSTN)
  • Uplink (UL): MS → BS · Downlink (DL): BS → MS
  • TX → Channel → RX: attenuation, phase shift, noise
✅ Key Wireless System Requirements
  • Bidirectional Communication: simultaneous send & receive
  • Location Independence: caller need not know UE location
  • Mobility: seamless handover across base stations during a call
  • Call Initiation: from user or network side
  • Privacy & Security: data intended only for the recipient
Cellular Principle & Frequency Reuse
🔢 Multiple Access
  • Number of active users limited by bandwidth
  • Users share bandwidth via multiple access schemes (FDMA, TDMA, CDMA, OFDMA)
  • FDD: uplink & downlink on separate frequencies
  • TDD: uplink & downlink share same frequency, separated in time
🗺️ Cellular Principle
  • Network area divided into smaller cells to maximize bandwidth use
  • Frequency Reuse: neighboring cells use different frequencies to avoid interference
  • Distant cells can reuse the same frequencies
  • Unlimited Mobility: users move seamlessly between cells (handover)
⚠️ Main Wireless Impairments
  • Multipath propagation: signal reaches RX via multiple paths
  • Fading: constructive/destructive interference → signal variations
  • Shadowing: obstacles reduce signal amplitude (large-scale)
  • ISI: inter-symbol interference in wideband systems
  • Spectrum limitations: regulated internationally by ITU/FCC/CEPT
💡 Key Idea of Chapter 1

Wireless communication has evolved from bulky analog 1G systems (1980s) to 5G ultra-low-latency networks. The cellular principle — dividing coverage into cells with frequency reuse — remains the core concept enabling scalable, mobile networks. The main challenges are multipath fading, shadowing, limited spectrum, and interference. Understanding these impairments and how to model/combat them is the foundation of the entire course.

Chapter 2 — Technical Challenges of Wireless Communications

Service requirements · Data rate · Range · Mobility · Energy · Multipath · Shadowing · ISI · Spectrum · Energy harvesting
🎯 Identify key design requirements per application type · Understand the main technical challenges in wireless system design
Requirements for Wireless Services
📶 Data Rate Requirements
  • Sensor Networks / IoT: few bits/s to 1 kbit/s; central nodes up to 10 Mbit/s
  • Speech Communications: 5 to 64 kbit/s
  • Computer Peripherals: ~1 Mbit/s
  • Video Streaming: 200 kbit/s → 20 Mbit/s; HDTV needs 2 Gbit/s
  • Web/Social Media: 1–10 Mbit/s with high peak rates
  • PANs / VR: 100 Mbit/s to 10 Gbit/s (up to 100 Gbit/s for VR)
📏 Range & Number of Users
  • BANs: 1 m range — body-area communication
  • PANs: up to 10 m — personal devices (Bluetooth)
  • WLANs: up to 100 m — ~10 simultaneous users (Wi-Fi)
  • Microcells: 300 m, 5–500 users
  • Macrocells: up to 30 km — wide-area coverage
  • Satellite: geostationary → 1000 km radius
Mobility & Energy Constraints
🚶 Mobility Classes
  • Fixed: always communicates from same location
  • Nomadic: stationary during use, moved between locations
  • Low Mobility: pedestrian speed — slow channel variation
  • High Mobility: 30–150 km/h (cars)
  • Extreme Mobility: 300–1000 km/h (trains/planes) — challenging handover
🔋 Energy Constraints
  • Battery-powered devices require energy efficiency
  • Energy Harvesting: converting ambient energy (RF, solar, thermal) to electrical power
  • Trade-off: high sensitivity ↔ energy efficiency
  • Design must balance data rate, range, and power consumption
📊 Spectrum Constraints
  • Wireless spectrum is limited and regulated internationally
  • Regulated by ITU, FCC (USA), ARIB (Japan), CEPT (Europe)
  • Spectrum is allocated in frequency bands per application
  • Efficient reuse strategies are essential (cellular, OFDMA, massive MIMO)
Core Technical Challenges
🌊 Multipath Propagation
  • Signal reaches RX via multiple paths due to reflections, diffractions, scattering
  • Different Multi-Path Components (MPCs) arrive at different delays and phases
  • Constructive interference: paths add up → signal boost
  • Destructive interference: paths cancel → deep fading dip
  • Results in small-scale fading (rapid amplitude variations over λ/2 distance)
🏢 Shadowing & ISI
  • Shadowing: obstacles (buildings, walls) block signal → large-scale amplitude reduction
  • Causes log-normal variation around the mean path loss
  • ISI (Inter-Symbol Interference): in wideband systems, delayed copies of a symbol interfere with the next symbol
  • Without countermeasures, ISI introduces irreducible error floors
  • Solutions: equalization, OFDM, guard intervals

💡 Key Idea of Chapter 2

Wireless system design requires balancing four competing requirements: data rate, range, mobility, and energy. No single technology is optimal for all. The core technical difficulties stem from the wireless channel itself: multipath fading causes rapid signal variations, shadowing causes slow amplitude drops, ISI limits bandwidth in wideband systems, and Doppler affects fast-moving users. Understanding these challenges motivates all the modeling and mitigation techniques covered in subsequent chapters.

Chapter 3 — Wireless System Design Overview

SNR basics · Noise sources · Noise factor · Equivalent noise · Antennas · EIRP · Link budget · Downlink vs Uplink · Coverage estimation
🎯 Compute SNR in a wireless link · Build a complete link budget · Estimate coverage distance from system parameters
Noise in a Wireless Receiver
🔊 Noise Sources
  • Thermal noise: due to random electron motion — unavoidable
  • Noise picked up by antenna: external interference and environmental noise
  • Man-made noise: spurious emissions from other systems
  • Total noise power in bandwidth B: Pn = −174 + 10·log₁₀(B) dBm
  • −174 dBm/Hz = thermal noise floor at room temperature (290 K)
📐 Noise Characterization
  • Power spectral density expressed in three ways:
  • 1) Directly [W/Hz] — linear scale
  • 2) Noise temperature [Kelvin] — T₀ = 290 K (room temperature)
  • 3) Noise factor F [dimensionless] — ratio of input SNR to output SNR
  • Relation: S = k · T₀ · F, where k = 1.38×10⁻²³ W/Hz (Boltzmann)
  • All noise sources replaced by a single equivalent noise source
Antennas & Radiated Power
📡 Isotropic Antenna
  • Radiates equally in all directions (spherical radiation pattern)
  • Theoretical reference — cannot be built physically
  • Antenna gain G is measured relative to the isotropic antenna
  • Dipole antenna: gains ≈ 2.15 dBi above isotropic
⚡ EIRP
  • EIRP = Equivalent Isotropically Radiated Power
  • EIRP [dBm] = P_TX [dBm] + G_TX [dBi]
  • Represents the power a lossless isotropic antenna would need to produce the same field
  • Antenna losses reduce effective gain (especially relevant for UE)
🔁 Uplink vs Downlink
  • UL and DL are reciprocal in path propagation terms
  • But differ in: transmit power, antenna gain, noise figure
  • BS antenna gain typically much higher than UE antenna
  • UE transmit power limited by battery → UL often more constrained
Link Budget
What is a Link Budget?
  • An accounting of all gains and losses in the signal path from TX to RX
  • All quantities expressed in dB / dBm (multiplicative factors become additions)
  • Output: required transmit power, or achievable coverage distance
  • Used for both Downlink (BS→UE) and Uplink (UE→BS)
P_RX [dBm] = P_TX [dBm] + G_TX [dBi] − L_path [dB] + G_RX [dBi] − L_losses [dB] SNR [dB] = P_RX [dBm] − P_noise [dBm] P_noise [dBm] = −174 + 10·log₁₀(B) + NF [dBm]
Path Loss Models (Mental Arithmetic)
🌌 Free-Space Reference (1 m, 1 GHz)
  • At 1 m distance, 1 GHz carrier: L_FS = 31.5 dB (useful reference)
  • For distances d > 1 m but d < d_break: add 20·log₁₀(d) dB
  • For d > d_break (two-ray region): add 40·log₁₀(d/d_break) extra
  • Frequency scaling: every doubling of f adds +6 dB to free-space loss
📏 Coverage Estimation Example
  • GSM downlink: f = 950 MHz, P_TX = 43 dBm, G_TX = 15 dBi
  • Sensitivity (RX min power) → determines max allowable path loss
  • With n = 3.5 (NLOS): coverage distance ≈ 8.8 km
  • Key: when n is given, solve 10n·log₁₀(d) = max path loss margin

💡 Key Idea of Chapter 3

A link budget is the central design tool: express all gains and losses in dB, sum them up, and check whether SNR ≥ minimum required SNR. The noise floor is set by −174 dBm/Hz + 10·log₁₀(B) + NF. EIRP = P_TX + G_TX captures the effective radiated power. Path loss dominates at large distances and scales as 20n·log₁₀(d) where n is the path loss exponent (n = 2 in free space, n ≈ 3.5 in typical outdoor NLOS). A fading margin must be added to handle random channel variations.

Chapter 4 — Propagation Effects

Free-space loss (Friis) · Far field · Reflection & transmission · TE/TM waves · Penetration loss · d⁻⁴ law · Diffraction · Fresnel zones · Multi-screen diffraction
🎯 Apply Friis' law · Compute reflection/transmission coefficients · Understand the d⁻⁴ power decay · Model diffraction past obstacles
Why Channel Modelling?
Motivation
  • Performance of a radio system is ultimately determined by the radio channel
  • Without reliable channel models → impossible to design algorithms, antennas, or systems that work in real environments
  • Channel models are the basis for: system design, algorithm design, antenna design, simulation
  • The radio channel is more than just a loss — it has behavior in time, frequency, space, delay, and direction
Basic Propagation Mechanisms
🌊 Free-Space Path Loss (Friis)
  • Received power with isotropic antennas:
  • P_RX = P_TX · G_TX · G_RX · (λ / 4πd)²
  • In dB: P_RX = P_TX + G_TX + G_RX − 20·log₁₀(4πd/λ)
  • Valid only in the far field (Rayleigh distance > 2L²/λ)
  • Power decreases as d⁻² in free space
🔁 Reflection & Transmission
  • When a radio wave hits a material boundary: reflection, transmission, absorption
  • Must distinguish TE (transverse electric) and TM (transverse magnetic) waves
  • Reflection angle = angle of incidence (Snell's law)
  • Brewster angle: TM wave has zero reflection
  • Phase inversion at grazing angle for TE polarization
🏗️ Penetration Loss Through Walls
  • Transmission through a wall = layered dielectric structure
  • Effective penetration loss is a function of frequency
  • Strongly depends on building material
  • Steel concrete & energy-saving windows: high attenuation at 2 GHz
  • Essentially impenetrable at mm-wave frequencies (5G)
The d⁻⁴ Power Law (Two-Ray Model)
📉 Two-Ray Ground Reflection
  • Scenario: direct ray + ground-reflected ray
  • Below breakpoint distance d_break: oscillatory behavior (ups and downs)
  • Beyond d_break: power decays as d⁻⁴ (instead of d⁻² in free space)
  • Breakpoint: d_break = 4·h_TX · h_RX / λ
P_RX ∝ (h_TX · h_RX)² / d⁴ for d >> d_break
📐 Log-Scale Path Loss Model
  • General model: PL(d) = PL(d₀) + 10n·log₁₀(d/d₀) [dB]
  • n = path loss exponent: 2 (free space), 3.5–4.5 (NLOS urban)
  • d₀ = reference distance (typically 1 m)
  • Smooth decay after the breakpoint (no oscillations in average)
  • Real-world: n = 1.5–2.5 (LOS), n = 3.5–4.5 (NLOS)
Diffraction
🏔️ Single-Screen Diffraction
  • Based on Huygens' principle: each point of the wavefront is a source of secondary waves
  • Semi-infinite screen: diffraction field computed from knife-edge model
  • Fresnel zones: ellipsoidal regions — obstruction of Fresnel zones causes diffraction loss
  • Loss increases rapidly when the obstacle enters the first Fresnel zone
🏙️ Multiple Screens
  • Urban environments: multiple buildings between TX and RX
  • Bullington's method: replaces multiple screens with a single equivalent screen
  • Epstein–Peterson method: considers each screen individually — places virtual TX/RX at screen ends
  • Each screen's diffraction loss computed separately, then summed
  • Causes power to decay more rapidly than free space
💡 Key Idea of Chapter 4

The radio channel is shaped by five basic mechanisms: free-space loss, reflection, transmission, diffraction, and diffuse scattering. Friis' law gives free-space d⁻² decay. The two-ray model predicts d⁻⁴ decay beyond the breakpoint — a much harsher decay. Real-world channels combine all mechanisms. Diffraction over rooftops and walls is critical in urban environments and is modeled using Fresnel zones and knife-edge or multi-screen models. All of these mechanisms contribute to the channel impulse response studied in later chapters.

Chapter 5 — Statistical Channel Modeling

Large-scale vs small-scale · Small-scale fading · Rayleigh distribution · Rice distribution · Fading margin · Outage probability · Shadowing
🎯 Distinguish large-scale from small-scale fading · Derive Rayleigh/Rice distributions · Compute fading margins · Quantify outage probability
Large-Scale vs Small-Scale Fading
📐 Channel Representation
  • Narrowband complex channel: y(t) = A(t)·exp(jφ(t)) · x(t)
  • Attenuation A(t) and phase φ(t) are what we model statistically
  • Large-scale fading: path loss + shadowing (slow variation over 10–100 m)
  • Small-scale fading: multipath interference (rapid variation over λ/2 ≈ cm)
  • Both superimposed on top of each other in received power
🔍 What Causes Small-Scale Fading
  • Multiple incoming waves with independent amplitudes and random phases
  • Sum of phasors: Re and Im components are sums of many i.i.d. components
  • By the Central Limit Theorem: Re and Im → independent Gaussian (zero mean)
  • Therefore: amplitude follows a Rayleigh distribution (no LOS)
  • Phase is uniformly distributed on [0, 2π]
Rayleigh Fading
📊 Rayleigh Distribution
  • Valid when: no dominant LOS component (NLOS scenario)
  • 2D Gaussian tap → amplitude follows Rayleigh PDF
  • PDF: p(r) = (r/σ²) · exp(−r²/2σ²), r ≥ 0
  • RMS value: r_rms = σ√2
  • Outage probability: Pr(r < r_min) = 1 − exp(−r_min²/r_rms²)
🛡️ Fading Margin
  • Mean received power (link budget) is only guaranteed 50% of the time
  • Must add a fading margin (FM) to ensure coverage in e.g. 90% or 99% of locations
  • For 1% outage with Rayleigh fading: FM ≈ 20 dB
  • Derivation: solve Pr(r < r_min) = 0.01 for required r_min → margin = 10·log₁₀(r_rms/r_min)
  • Key insight: Rayleigh fading requires large margins due to deep fading dips
Rice Fading & Shadowing
☀️ Rice Fading (LOS Scenario)
  • One dominant LOS component + many scattered components
  • Amplitude follows Rice (Rician) distribution
  • K-factor: ratio of LOS power to scattered power (K = A²/2σ²)
  • K = 0 → Rayleigh · K → ∞ → AWGN (no fading)
  • Phase: not uniform (biased toward LOS direction)
  • Rice fading: less severe than Rayleigh (LOS component stabilizes the link)
🏢 Shadowing (Large-Scale)
  • Buildings and terrain cause random amplitude variations around mean path loss
  • Modeled as log-normal: S_dB ~ N(0, σ_S²) where σ_S ≈ 4–10 dB
  • Correlation distance: shadowing is correlated over 10–100 m
  • Combined model: PL_dB(d) = PL̄_dB(d) + S_dB
  • Both fading margin (small-scale) and shadowing margin needed in link budget
Rayleigh Fading with Interference
📉 Statistics of Fading Dips
  • Frequency of fading dips depends on Doppler frequency (speed)
  • Length of fading dips depends on vehicle speed and dip depth
  • Fast-moving UE → many short dips; slow-moving → fewer but longer dips
📊 SIR Under Rayleigh Fading
  • Desired signal AND interferer both Rayleigh-faded
  • Probability that instantaneous SIR < 0 dB when mean SIR = 10 dB: computable from ratio of exponential random variables
  • Key result: Rayleigh fading significantly degrades interference statistics vs AWGN
💡 Key Idea of Chapter 5

The received signal amplitude is modeled statistically: Rayleigh (no LOS, NLOS) or Rice (with LOS). Rayleigh fading is the worst case and requires large fading margins (≈20 dB for 1% outage). The log-normal shadowing adds a separate, slower variation. The link budget must account for both margins. The key mathematical insight is that summing many independent multipath components leads to Gaussian complex amplitudes → Rayleigh envelope — a direct consequence of the Central Limit Theorem.

Chapter 6 — Wideband Channels & Mathematical Foundations

Two-path model · Delay dispersion · Frequency selectivity · System functions · WSSUS model · Delay spread · Coherence bandwidth · Doppler spread · Coherence time · Directional channels
🎯 Model wideband channels via transfer functions · Understand WSSUS · Compute condensed channel parameters
From Narrowband to Wideband — The Two-Path Model
⚡ Two-Path Channel Impulse Response
  • Channel impulse response: h(τ) = a₁δ(τ−τ₁) + a₂δ(τ−τ₂)
  • aₖ = |aₖ|e^(jφₖ) : complex amplitude of path k
  • τₖ = dₖ/c₀ : delay of path k (path length / speed of light)
  • Static environment → channel is LTI (Linear Time-Invariant)
📊 Transfer Function & Frequency Selectivity
  • H(f) = Fourier transform of h(τ): H(f) = a₁e^(−j2πfτ₁) + a₂e^(−j2πfτ₂)
  • |H(f)|² = |a₁|² + |a₂|² + 2|a₁||a₂|cos(2πf·Δτ − Δφ)
  • Frequency-selective fading: different frequencies experience different gains
  • Notch spacing: Δf_notch = 1/Δτ (where Δτ = τ₂ − τ₁)
  • Group delay: τ_Gr = −(1/2π)·d(arg H(f))/df — large at fading dips → ISI
General Wideband Channel — System Functions
🔄 Four Equivalent Descriptions
  • 1) Time-variant impulse response h(t,τ): fundamental description
  • 2) Time-variant transfer function H(t,f): Fourier of h over τ
  • 3) Spreading function S(ν,τ): Fourier of h over t → Doppler domain
  • 4) Doppler-variant spreading function B(ν,f): double Fourier transform
  • Due to movement, impulse response changes with time
  • Y(f) = X(f)·H(f) only valid for slowly time-varying channels
🔁 Delay–Doppler Duality
  • Mathematical equivalence between frequency variation and time variation:
  • Wideband: H(f) varies with f due to path delays τₗ → phase e^(−j2πfτₗ)
  • Narrowband: H(t) varies with t due to Doppler shifts νₗ → phase e^(j2πνₗt)
  • Delay ↔ Doppler duality: same mathematical structure
  • A 2π phase shift occurs over: Δf = 1/τₗ (delay) or Δt = 1/νₗ (Doppler)
WSSUS Model & Stochastic System Functions
📐 WSSUS Assumptions
  • WSS: Wide-Sense Stationary — channel statistics don't change with time
  • US: Uncorrelated Scattering — different delays are uncorrelated
  • ACF of h(t,τ) depends only on Δt and τ (not absolute time t)
  • Most practical channel models assume WSSUS for tractability
📊 Power Delay Profile
  • P(τ) = ACF of h(t,τ) at Δt = 0 — power as function of delay
  • Shows how received power is distributed across delay bins
  • Integral of P(τ) = total received power
  • Basis for computing all condensed parameters
🌐 Directional Channels (MIMO)
  • For MIMO/multi-antenna: channel depends on direction of arrival and departure
  • Angular spread: second central moment of angular power spectrum
  • Connection: SISO = integral of MIMO channel over all directions
  • Fleury definition handles angular ambiguity due to periodicity

💡 Key Idea of Chapter 6

Wideband channels are described by the time-variant impulse response h(t,τ) with four equivalent system function representations. The WSSUS model simplifies analysis by assuming stationarity in time and uncorrelated scattering across delays. The four key condensed parameters are: RMS delay spread (characterizes ISI), coherence bandwidth (frequency range of flat fading), Doppler spread (channel time-variation speed), and coherence time (how long the channel stays constant). These parameters directly govern the choice of modulation scheme, guard interval (OFDM), and pilot density.

Chapter 7 — Channel Models

Location-specific vs stochastic models · Pathloss models · Floating-intercept model · CI model · LOS/NLOS parameters · Shadowing · Standardized models (CDMA, OFDM) · Tapped-delay-line
🎯 Select the right channel model for each use case · Fit pathloss parameters · Apply standardized models for system simulation
Two Types of Channel Models
🗺️ Location-Specific Models
  • Used for network planning and deployment
  • Rely on detailed geographic and morphological data
  • Derived from ray-tracing or ray-launching simulations
  • High accuracy for a specific environment but not generalizable
  • Computationally expensive — not suitable for algorithm design
🎲 Stochastic Models
  • Used for design, testing, and type approval
  • Represent channel impulse response with a small number of parameters
  • Location-independent — applicable anywhere of the same environment type
  • Standardized by 3GPP, ITU, IEEE → reproducibility across implementations
  • Model development is measurement-based
Pathloss Models
📐 Floating-Intercept (FI) Model
  • General log-distance model:
  • PL_dB(d) = α_PL · 10·log₁₀(d/d₀) + β_PL
  • α_PL = slope (path loss exponent)
  • β_PL = path loss at reference distance d₀
  • Both parameters are fitted from measurements
  • Valid only within the measured distance range
📏 Close-In (CI) Model
  • Sets reference pathloss to the free-space value:
  • PL_dB(d) = α_PL,CI · 10·log₁₀(d/d₀) + PL_FS(d₀)
  • Only α is fitted — PL_FS(d₀) is known theoretically
  • Easier comparison across different frequency measurements
  • More physically motivated than the FI model

Standardized Channel Models
📡 Tapped-Delay-Line (TDL) Model
  • Channel modeled as FIR filter with random tap coefficients
  • Each tap = one resolvable multipath cluster
  • Tap delays: fixed · Tap amplitudes: random, Rayleigh or Rice
  • Tap powers follow the Power Delay Profile
  • Each tap time-varies as a narrowband fading process (Jakes model)
🔊 CDMA / 3G Models
  • ITU-R and 3GPP defined standardized profiles for 3G system evaluation
  • Typical: Pedestrian A/B, Vehicular A/B
  • Defined by: tap delays, tap powers, Doppler spectrum shape
  • Used in chip-level simulations for CDMA receiver design
🌐 OFDM / 4G–5G Models
  • 3GPP defines CDL (Clustered Delay Line) and TDL models
  • CDL models: directional, with clusters of MPCs at specific angles and delays
  • NR (5G) models: cover sub-6 GHz to mmWave bands
  • Basis for all 4G/5G link- and system-level simulations
  • OFDM uses CP length based on max delay spread of model
Shadowing Model & Combined Path Loss
🏢 Log-Normal Shadowing
  • Total path gain = deterministic mean + random shadowing:
  • PG_dB(d) = P̄G_dB(d) + S_dB
  • S_dB ~ N(0, σ_S²) — Gaussian in dB scale (log-normal in linear)
  • Pathloss = −PG_dB(d)
  • In urban environments: Manhattan distance may be more relevant than Euclidean
📊 Directional Pathloss
  • For directional antennas: pathloss depends on antenna patterns
  • Directional path gain = max received power over all antenna orientations minus antenna gain
  • Essential for high-frequency (mmWave) channels where beamforming is needed
  • MIMO: must model pathloss separately per antenna pair (full channel matrix)
💡 Key Idea of Chapter 7

Channel models translate propagation physics into usable simulation inputs. Two main types: location-specific (ray-tracing, for deployment planning) and stochastic (parametric TDL/CDL, for algorithm design and standardized testing). The floating-intercept and CI pathloss models fit measurement data with one or two parameters. Shadowing is always added as a log-normal random variable (σ_S ≈ 4–10 dB in urban). For system simulations, standardized models (3GPP TDL-A/B/C, CDL-A/B/C) are essential for reproducible, fair comparison between different implementations and algorithms.
